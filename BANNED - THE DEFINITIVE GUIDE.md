

## BANNED: THE DEFINITIVE GUIDE

## INTRODUCTION
This document is a personal style guide—a catalog of words, phrases, constructions, and
patterns that I don't want to see in my own writing. It exists because I got tired of catching the
same problems in revision, and because working with AI tools requires explicit boundaries to
prevent regression to generic, statistically-average prose.
I'm a content maximalist and a militant anti-tropist. My tolerance for cliché is low. My list of
banned elements is long. Some of what's cataloged here represents genuinely weak
writing—vague placeholders, dead metaphors, mechanical rhythms that substitute form for
content. But some of it is simply personal preference: common literary constructs that work fine
for other writers but that I find grating, overused, or tonally wrong for my projects.
This is not a universal prescription. It's not "if you use these, you're a bad writer." It's "if I use
these, I've gotten lazy, and I need to fix it."
The document is organized into four parts:
● Part 1: Constructions — Syntactic and structural patterns to avoid
● Part 2: Words and Phrases — Specific vocabulary and phrase-level bans
● Part 3: Guidelines — Principles, protocols, and revision checklists
● Part 4: Erotica-Specific — Additional rules governing explicit content
Use what applies. Ignore what doesn't. Adapt freely. The goal isn't compliance with my
preferences—it's clarity about your own. If nothing else, this document might help you identify
patterns you've never noticed, so you can decide for yourself whether they serve your work or
undermine it.
Chew the meat. Spit out the bones.

## PART 1: CONSTRUCTIONS
## Purpose

This section catalogs syntactic and structural patterns that weaken prose through mechanical
repetition, false rhythm, or decorative vagueness. These are not word-level problems—they're
architectural habits that simulate depth, tension, or emotion without creating it.
Core Principle: Every construction listed here substitutes form for content. The fix is always the
same: name what's actually happening, show consequence, or cut.

## 1.1 SEQUENTIAL ACTION PAIRS
Pattern: "X, then Y"
## What It Looks Like:
● "He stands, then sits."
● "She speaks, then falls silent."
● "He reaches out, then pulls back."
## Why It Fails:
● Creates robotic pacing—every action reads identically
● Removes emotional transition between gestures
● Reads as stage direction rather than lived movement
Flagging Trigger: If you write ", then" between two actions, pause. Ask: Does this sequence
reveal psychology, or am I just choreographing motion?

## 1.2 VAGUE INTERIORITY PLACEHOLDER
Pattern: "Something [verbs] in/behind [body part]"
## What It Looks Like:
● "Something shifts behind his eyes."
● "Something tightens in her chest."
● "Something flickers in his expression."
● "Something hardens in her voice."
## Why It Fails:
● "Something" signals the writer knows an effect occurred but can't specify what
● Treats emotion as mysterious rather than observable

● Creates false interiority—tells reader change happened without showing how
Flagging Trigger: If you write "something," stop. Ask: What exactly is happening? What would
an observer see or the character feel?

## 1.3 ANTHROPOMORPHIZED SILENCE
Pattern: "The silence [verbs]"
## What It Looks Like:
● "The silence stretches between them."
● "The silence sits heavy in the room."
● "The silence grows uncomfortable."
● "The silence hangs, thick and suffocating."
## Why It Fails:
● Turns silence into an actor rather than showing its effect
● Empty beat that simulates tension without creating it
● Stops momentum by naming atmosphere instead of dramatizing consequence
Flagging Trigger: If silence "does" something (stretches, sits, presses, hangs), rewrite to show
who breaks it, who endures it, what it costs.

## 1.4 NEGATION FORMULA
Pattern: "Not X, but Y"
## What It Looks Like:
● "Not angry, but resigned."
● "Not a question, but a statement."
● "Not love, but something close."
● "Not fear, but caution."
## Why It Fails:
● Hedges instead of commits
● Signals authorial indecision
● Becomes rhythmic tic—every nuanced feeling gets the same syntactic treatment

Flagging Trigger: If you write "not [X], but [Y]," ask: What is this actually? Can I name it
directly?

## 1.5 TRIPLE-BEAT LISTS
Pattern: Three-part noun/fragment sequences for emphasis
## What It Looks Like:
● "The exhaustion. The loneliness. The endurance."
● "The wine. The laughter. The silence."
● "The blood. The breath. The waiting."
## Why It Fails:
● Mimics poetic seriousness through structure, not content
● Halts narrative flow for rhetorical effect
● Once overused, becomes self-parody
Flagging Trigger: If you write three nouns/fragments in sequence for effect, ask: Am I building
tension, or performing it?

## 1.6 STACCATO VERB FRAGMENTS
Pattern: "[Verb]. [Verb]. [Verb]."
## What It Looks Like:
● "Stops. Reaches. Retreats."
● "Looks. Waits. Breathes."
● "Turns. Pauses. Leaves."
## Why It Fails:
● Overused in high-emotion beats until robotic
● Creates artificial pacing, erasing natural movement
● Pattern recognition kills power
Flagging Trigger: If you write three single-word verb sentences in a row, ask: Am I building
tension, or mimicking it?


## 1.7 CONJUNCTION STARTS (OVERUSE)
Pattern: Starting sentences with "And" or "But" for performative rhythm
## What It Looks Like:
● "And she knows he's lying."
● "But she can't walk away."
● "And still, he waits."
## Why It Fails:
● Over-reliance flattens voice
● Creates false intimacy through affected cadence
● Loses impact through repetition
Flagging Trigger: If you start with "And" or "But," ask: Does this sentence need dramatic
separation, or am I forcing rhythm?

## 1.8 STANDALONE "BECAUSE" FRAGMENTS
Pattern: "Because [explanation]." as its own sentence
## What It Looks Like:
● "Because she can't bear to look."
● "Because it's easier than lying."
● "Because he's already gone."
## Why It Fails:
● Imitates intimacy but signals shorthand thinking
● Backfill explanation instead of lived cause
● Tacks meaning onto flat line
Flagging Trigger: If you write "Because [X]" as a fragment, ask: Can I show this reason through
action instead?


## 1.9 HEDGED REACTIONS
Pattern: "A [reaction] that isn't quite [itself]"
## What It Looks Like:
● "A laugh that isn't quite a laugh."
● "A smile that isn't quite a smile."
● "A sigh that isn't quite a sigh."
● "A nod that isn't really agreement."
## Why It Fails:
● Creates emotional static—reader can't visualize or feel the gesture
● Substitutes contradiction for depth
● Becomes nervous tic once pattern is recognized
Flagging Trigger: If you write "isn't quite [X]," stop. Ask: What is this gesture actually doing?
What does it look like?

## 1.10 ECHO-LINE POETICS
Pattern: Consecutive sentences with parallel structure for false depth
## What It Looks Like:
● "He wanted to be touched. He wanted to be seen."
● "She wasn't angry. She wasn't anything."
● "It wasn't the silence. It was the waiting inside it."
● "It hurt. God, it hurt."
## Why It Fails:
● Artificial lyricism masking conceptual vacancy
● Emotional tautology—repetition replaces actual thought or development
● Second line rarely adds new information; just rephrases first line in adjacent terms
Flagging Trigger: If you write two consecutive sentences with mirrored structure, ask: Does the
second line develop the first, or am I just creating rhythm?

## 1.11 MOOD-PROP NEGATION

Pattern: Character performs action immediately negated as meaningless
## What It Looks Like:
● "She grabs the soy sauce she doesn't need."
● "He smoked a cigarette he didn't want."
● "She stared at a scratch he didn't make."
● "He drank tea he didn't really want."
## Why It Fails:
● Signals complexity through subtraction, not concrete choice
● Pretends specificity while revealing nothing
● Becomes filler, avoiding real conflict
Flagging Trigger: If you write "[action] + [negation]," ask: Why is this character doing this?
What does it reveal? If nothing, cut.

## 1.12 META-NARRATIVE INTRUSION
Pattern: Narration referencing story mechanics from within the story
## What It Looks Like:
● "Scene's not over."
● "He waited for the next beat."
● "This was the real moment."
● "She knew the conversation wasn't finished."
● "The air felt like something was about to happen."
## Why It Fails:
● Breaks immersion—makes reader conscious of author's hand
● Underlines narrative shape instead of letting it emerge
Flagging Trigger: If you write about "the scene" or "the moment" as structure, stop. You've left
character POV. Reenter through action.

## 1.13 ATMOSPHERIC FRONT-LOADING
Pattern: Scene opens with weather, skyline, or architecture before character presence

## What It Looks Like:
● "The New York skyline glowed through the penthouse window, casting neon over the
glass table."
● "Rain traced the windows of the Tokyo apartment, the city lights flickering below."
● "The waves crashed against the shore outside, the moonlight pooling across the floor."
## Why It Fails:
● Begins with set dressing, not story
● Creates distance when immediacy needed
● Mimics film language rather than character consciousness
● Filler buying time before committing to actual behavior
Flagging Trigger: If your first sentence could be read over a slow pan in a movie trailer, cut it.
Start with character.

## 1.14 GRAVITATIONAL METAPHORS
Pattern: Physics language for emotional connection
## What It Looks Like:
● "Pulled toward her."
● "Drawn to him."
● "Orbits around her."
● "The gravity between them."
● "Magnetic."
## Why It Fails:
● Turns attraction into automatism—robs relationships of will
● Default metaphor for romantic connection
● Ignores psychology in favor of pseudo-science
Flagging Trigger: If you write attraction as physics, stop. Make it a choice.

## 1.15 SUSPENSION PHRASES
Pattern: "Hangs in the air" / "hangs between them"

## What It Looks Like:
● "The words hang in the air."
● "The silence hangs between them."
● "The question hangs, unanswered."
● "The accusation hangs there."
## Why It Fails:
● Repetition numbs impact
● "Hanging" creates stasis, not consequence
● Suggests unresolved tension without showing what happens next
Flagging Trigger: If you write "hangs," ask: What happens because of this
silence/question/moment? Show aftermath, not suspended atmosphere.

## 1.16 IMPACT SIMILES
Pattern: "Like a [physical force]" for emotional shock
## What It Looks Like:
● "Hit like a physical blow."
● "Hit like a punch to the gut."
● "Hit like a freight train."
● "Landed like a slap."
● "Knocked the air out of him."
## Why It Fails:
● Relies on cliché rather than visceral description
● Makes every revelation feel identical
● Removes character-specific response
Flagging Trigger: If you write "like a blow," stop. Show the actual physical sensation instead.

## 1.17 RIPPLE/STILLNESS SIMILES
Pattern: Water imagery for emotional impact
## What It Looks Like:

● "Like a stone dropped in still water."
● "Rippled through him."
● "Sent ripples through the room."
● "The stillness shattered."
● "Cracked the silence like glass."
## Why It Fails:
● Dead simile universalized to meaninglessness
● No unique sensory or emotional anchor
● So common it's invisible
Flagging Trigger: If you write "like a stone" or "rippled through," cut it. Show impact directly.

## 1.18 PRECISION/CONTROL CLUSTER
Pattern: Overuse of clinical competence descriptors
## What It Looks Like:
● "Surgical precision."
● "With practiced ease."
● "Economical movement."
● "Calculated."
● "Calibrated."
● "Methodical."
● "Clinical."
● "Exacting."
## Why It Fails:
● Reduces character to competence performance
● Becomes shorthand for "impressive" without showing what's actually happening
● Flattens distinct characters into same cool efficiency
Flagging Trigger: If you describe action as "precise" or "surgical," ask: What does this actually
look like? What's the specific movement?

## 1.19 QUALITY/TEXTURE DEFAULTS
Pattern: Same material metaphors for voice, tension, or restraint

## What It Looks Like:
● "Velvet" (voice, darkness, threat)
● "Silk over steel"
● "Steel" (spine, voice, gaze, grip)
● "Iron" (control, grip, will)
● "Granite"
● "Marble"
## Why It Fails:
● So overused they're invisible
● Every controlled character gets the same metallurgical description
● No longer creates sensory impression
Flagging Trigger: If you reach for steel/iron/velvet/silk, find a different texture entirely—or
describe the actual sensation.

## 1.20 BLANK DESIRE STATEMENTS
Pattern: Desire expressed as appetite without psychological grounding
## What It Looks Like:
● "He wanted her. God, he wanted her."
● "She needed him like air."
● "The wanting was unbearable."
● "He ached for her."
● "She craved him."
## Why It Fails:
● Desire without motive reads as template—could apply to any character in any story
● Reduces complex desire to generic biological drive
● No differentiation between characters
Flagging Trigger: If you write "wanted her" or "needed him," ask: Wanted/needed what,
specifically? For what purpose? What does this desire reveal?

## 1.21 ARTICULATION BY PROXY

Pattern: Claiming communication happened without showing content
## What It Looks Like:
● "He gave her a look that said everything."
● "They didn't need words."
● "She knew what he meant."
● "The look they exchanged spoke volumes."
● "His expression said it all."
## Why It Fails:
● Admits narrative laziness—writer doesn't know what's being communicated
● Pretends at intimacy while withholding actual subtext
● Breaks the contract with reader: if communication happens, we should understand its
content
Flagging Trigger: If you write "a look that said" or "didn't need words," ask: What is actually
being communicated here? Can I show the exchange?

## 1.22 HOLLOW RESTRAINT
Pattern: Describing containment without naming what's contained or what it costs
## What It Looks Like:
● "He held it together."
● "She swallowed it down."
● "He kept it inside."
● "She bottled it up."
● "He locked it away."
## Why It Fails:
● Vague abstraction of repression without psychological texture
● Generic across all characters—everyone "holds it together" identically
● What matters is what leaks when containment fails
Flagging Trigger: If you write about holding/containing emotion, ask: What exactly is being
held? How is this character physically achieving restraint? What breaks if they stop?


## 1.23 AESTHETICIZED DAMAGE
Pattern: Stylized dysfunction as character shorthand
## What It Looks Like:
● "He poured another drink he didn't need."
● "She hadn't slept in three days."
● "The city ate people like him alive."
● "Another sleepless night, another empty bottle."
● "She lived on coffee and cigarettes and spite."
## Why It Fails:
● Romanticizes burnout and alienation as aesthetic rather than lived reality
● Substitutes darkness for depth
● Performs suffering without dramatizing what it costs
Flagging Trigger: If you write about drinking, sleeplessness, or self-destruction, ask: Am I
showing what this costs, or am I using it as character decoration?

## 1.24 FAUX-INTELLECTUAL APHORISM
Pattern: Lines that sound profound but offer zero narrative content
## What It Looks Like:
● "We are all just stories in the end."
● "Maybe love was the real war."
● "Sometimes, survival looks like surrender."
● "Pain is just love with nowhere to go."
● "The heart wants what it wants, even when it knows better."
## Why It Fails:
● Performs insight without grounding in character experience
● Breaks tonal realism—no one thinks in quotable one-liners during actual emotional crisis
● Stops narrative momentum to deliver manufactured profundity
Flagging Trigger: If you write a line that sounds like it belongs on a motivational poster or could
be retweeted as "deep thoughts," stop. Ask: Is this a real thought this specific character would
have in this moment?


## 1.25 MISAPPLIED EPIC TONE
Pattern: Heightened language treating minor moments as climactic
## What It Looks Like:
● "In that instant, something in him broke forever."
● "The world held its breath."
● "It was only a touch, but it changed everything."
● "Nothing would ever be the same."
## Why It Fails:
● Every moment reads as climax—undermines proportion
● Sounds algorithmically "important" rather than earned
● Breaks psychological realism
Flagging Trigger: If you write "changed everything" or "broke forever," ask: Is this moment
actually that big? If not, scale down.

## 1.26 ELEGANT VARIATION
Pattern: Cycling through synonyms or descriptors to avoid repeating a character's name
## What It Looks Like:
● "the older man" / "the younger woman" rotating with names
● "the Japanese man" / "the dark-skinned woman" substituting for names
● "his lover" / "her partner" / "the other man" used interchangeably
● "the protagonist" / "the key player" / "the central figure"
## Why It Fails:
● Draws attention to the writer avoiding repetition rather than to the scene
● Creates distance—readers register descriptors as narrative intrusion
● Flattens character into demographic marker or role
● Signals AI generation through mechanical synonym cycling
Flagging Trigger: If you've used a descriptor instead of a name, ask: Would I notice this
rotation as a reader? Is the descriptor doing work (establishing POV, power dynamic, emotional
distance), or am I just avoiding the name?

The Exception: Descriptors are valid when they reveal POV or shift. "The man who used to be
her husband" is doing work. "The tall man" rotating with "[CHARACTER]" is not.

## 1.27 FALSE RANGE CONSTRUCTION
Pattern: "From X to Y" where X and Y aren't on the same measurable scale
## What It Looks Like:
● "From the intimacy of their first kiss to the architecture of their silence"
● "From heartbreak to revolution"
● "From the weight of history to the lightness of her laugh"
● "From pain to transformation to power"
## Why It Fails:
● Sounds comprehensive but means nothing
● No actual scale exists between the endpoints
● Borrowed from persuasive/promotional writing where vagueness is a feature
● Mimics profundity through syntactic shape rather than content
Flagging Trigger: If you write "from X to Y," ask: Can I identify a middle ground between these
endpoints without switching to a different scale? If no, it's a false range. Cut it.

## 1.28 SUPERFICIAL ANALYSIS AS NARRATION
Pattern: Present participle phrases attached to sentences to editorialize meaning
## What It Looks Like:
● "She left the room, highlighting her frustration."
● "He poured another drink, underscoring his exhaustion."
● "They sat in silence, reflecting the distance between them."
● "She touched his arm, emphasizing her concern."
## Why It Fails:
● Narrator steps in to explain what the action means
● Removes reader's role in interpreting behavior
● The action should carry its own weight—if it doesn't, the action is wrong
● Classic AI tell: inanimate facts/events "highlighting" or "underscoring" things

Flagging Trigger: If you attach a participle phrase that interprets the action (highlighting,
underscoring, reflecting, emphasizing, showcasing, symbolizing), delete it. The action either
speaks for itself or needs to be rewritten so it does.

## 1.29 NEGATIVE PARALLELISM
Pattern: "Not only... but..." or "It's not about X, it's about Y" constructions
## What It Looks Like:
● "It wasn't just the silence—it was everything the silence contained."
● "Not only had she lost him, but she had lost herself."
● "It's not about the money. It's about respect."
● "He didn't just leave. He erased himself."
## Why It Fails:
● Performs profundity through syntactic opposition
● The second clause often restates the first in inflated terms
● Halts narrative momentum for rhetorical emphasis
● Overused in AI output as a depth-simulation device
Flagging Trigger: If you write "not only... but" or "it's not about X, it's about Y," ask: Is the
second clause adding information, or just amplifying? Can I cut the construction and state the
point directly?

## 1.30 SIMILE-AS-ADVERB
Pattern: "With [noun] of someone [verb]ing"
## What It Looks Like:
● "With the grim determination of someone preparing for a siege."
● "With the weariness of someone who had explained this before."
● "With the caution of someone approaching a wild animal."
● "With the focus of someone defusing a bomb."
## Why It Fails:
● Sounds like characterization but it's just a simile doing an adverb's job
● Invents a hypothetical person to describe the actual person's state

● Unnecessarily wordy—just describe the tone or behavior directly
● Claude loves this construction because it feels specific without requiring actual specificity
Flagging Trigger: If you write "with the [noun] of someone," stop. Ask: Can I just describe what
they're actually doing or feeling? Do I need the imaginary siege-preparer?


## 1.31 TRAILING PARTICIPLE CONSTRUCTION
Pattern: [Main clause], [present participle phrase]—repeated until every sentence has the same
trailing rhythm.
## What It Looks Like:
● "Elara opened the box, her fingers trembling."
● "Inside was a necklace, the diamond pendant glimmering in the light."
● "She held it in the air, gazing at the luminous jewel."
● "He crossed the room, running a hand through his hair."
● "She sat down, pulling her knees to her chest."
● "He turned away, staring out the window."
## Why It Fails:
● Creates monotonous sentence rhythm—every action gets the same syntactic shape
● Often produces simultaneous actions that aren't actually simultaneous ("She opened the
door, stepping inside"—did she phase through it mid-swing?)
● Becomes a crutch for cramming extra detail into sentences without varying structure
● Gives prose a floaty, continuous quality where everything is happening at once in an
endless "-ing" stream
● Three of these in a row and the reader is swimming in participles
The Compounding Problem: One trailing participle is fine. Two in a paragraph is noticeable.
Three or more consecutive sentences with this structure and the prose starts to feel like it's
being generated rather than written. AI defaults to this rhythm because it's syntactically safe and
infinitely extensible.
Flagging Trigger: If you've written more than two sentences in a row ending with a participle
phrase, stop. Vary the structure—lead with the participle, use a period and make it its own
sentence, or cut the participle entirely and just describe the action directly.

## PART 2: WORDS AND PHRASES
## Purpose
This section catalogs specific words and phrases that weaken prose through overuse,
vagueness, or cliché. These are the building blocks of the structural problems in Part 1—the
individual terms that signal lazy writing, generic emotion, or AI-generated filler.
Core Principle: If a word or phrase could appear unchanged across any character, any genre,
any story—it's doing no work. Cut it.

## 2.1 PHYSICAL TELLS (EMOTION SUBSTITUTES)
These get used reflexively instead of actual interiority, psychology, or consequence. They flatten
distinct characters into identical nervous systems.
Jaw/Face
● jaw tightens / clenches / sets / locks
● jaw works
● muscle jumps / ticks in jaw
● teeth grind
Throat/Breath
● throat works / bobs
● swallows hard / thickly
● breath catches / hitches / stutters
● breath punched out
● exhales slowly / shakily

● releases a breath [he/she] didn't know [he/she] was holding
● forgets to breathe
## Eyes
● eyes darken / go dark
● pupils blown / blown wide
● gaze sharpens / hardens / softens
● something flickers in [his/her] eyes
● eyes search [his/her] face
Hands/Body
● hands curl into fists
● knuckles whiten
● fingers flex / twitch at [his/her] side
● hand flexes at side
● spine straightens / stiffens
● shoulders tense / drop
● goes very still
● freezes
Heart/Chest
● heart stutters / pounds / races
● chest tightens / aches
● something cracks / shifts / loosens in [his/her] chest
Why These Fail: Autonomic responses don't differentiate emotion. Every character ends up
with the same body language regardless of psychology, history, or coping patterns.

## 2.2 VAGUE INTERIORITY PLACEHOLDERS
These substitute for naming actual emotions or stakes.
● something in [him/her] shifts / breaks / cracks / loosens / changes
● something unnameable
● something like [emotion]
● the weight of [X] settles / presses / lands
● a wave of [emotion] crashes / washes over
● [emotion] curls / coils in [his/her] stomach
● feels it in [his/her] bones

● the words hang between them
● the silence stretches / sits heavy / presses
● the air thickens / shifts / changes
● the room feels smaller
Why These Fail: "Something" signals the writer knows an effect occurred but can't specify
what. These phrases tell that change happened without showing how.

## 2.3 DEFAULT EMOTION DESCRIPTORS
Overused intensity markers that have lost all specificity.
● raw
● visceral
● primal
● bone-deep
● soul-deep
● marrow-deep
● paper-thin (control, patience, composure)
● gossamer-thin
● razor-thin
● threadbare
● frayed edges
● worn thin
● stretched to breaking
Why These Fail: These words affect intensity without specifying quality. "Raw" anger and "raw"
grief and "raw" desire all collapse into the same vague urgency.

## 2.4 TRANSITION / BEAT PLACEHOLDERS
Empty time markers simulating pacing without creating it.
● for a long moment
● for a beat
● for a suspended moment
● after a moment
● a pause, then
● finally (as transition)
● when [he/she] speaks, [his/her] voice is [adjective]

● the silence that follows is [adjective]
Why These Fail: These phrases label time passing rather than showing what happens during it.
They're stage directions, not lived experience.

## 2.8 COMPETENCE PORN DESCRIPTORS
Shorthand for capability that tells rather than shows.

● fluid
● graceful economy

● quiet competence
● easy confidence
● contained power
● coiled energy

## PART 3: GUIDELINES
## Purpose
This section provides the operational framework for applying Parts 1 and 2. It explains why
these bans exist, what to do instead, and how to systematically check your work.
Core Principle: Every banned element shares one root cause—substituting form for content,
decoration for precision, or familiar rhythm for earned meaning. The corrective is always the
same: be specific, be embodied, show consequence.


## 3.1 THE ROOT PROBLEM
All banned constructions, words, and phrases fail for the same reasons:
## They Simulate Rather Than Create
● Rhythm that sounds literary but carries no meaning
● Gestures that look like emotion but specify nothing
● Metaphors that feel poetic but clarify nothing
## They Are Interchangeable
● Could appear in any story, any genre, any character
● Reveal nothing specific about this person in this moment
● Function as placeholder until the writer figures out what's actually happening
## They Tell Rather Than Show
● Label that emotion occurred rather than dramatizing it
● Announce that time passed rather than showing what filled it
● Declare that something changed rather than making the change visible
They Default to Familiar
● Reach for the first phrase that comes to mind
● Rely on reader recognition of trope rather than earned response
● Substitute genre convention for character-specific behavior
## The Accumulation Principle
No single banned element makes prose bad. One "jaw tightens" is fine. One trailing participle is
fine. One "something shifted" might even be the right choice in context.
The problem is accumulation.
When the same constructions, the same physical tells, the same vague interiority placeholders
appear over and over—across characters, across scenes, regardless of context—the prose
flattens. It stops feeling like a specific story about specific people and starts feeling like it was
assembled from a bag of interchangeable parts.
This is the actual AI tell. Not any single word or phrase, but the density of defaults. AI writing
sounds like AI writing because it reaches for the statistically common option every time. It

doesn't know to vary. It doesn't know that one character's stress response should look different
from another's. It doesn't know that the third "silence stretched between them" in a chapter has
lost all power. It just keeps generating the highest-probability completion.
Human writers fall into the same trap—reaching for familiar constructions on autopilot,
defaulting to the first phrase that comes to mind. The result is identical: prose that reads as
generated rather than written.
## The Test:
When you catch a banned element, don't just ask "is this bad?" Ask:
● How many times have I used this in the last ten pages?
● Have I used it for multiple characters interchangeably?
● Is this the first appearance (possibly fine) or the fifth (definitely a pattern)?
A single "the weight of it settled in his chest" might work. The third one in a chapter means
you're leaning on a crutch.
## The Corollary:
This also means witch-hunting individual constructions is pointless. Someone who flags every
em-dash or every "And" sentence-start as "AI writing" has missed the point. The question isn't
whether a construction appears—it's whether it appears reflexively, repeatedly, and
interchangeably.
Density. Pattern. Accumulation. That's what to watch for.


## 3.2 THE CORRECTIVE FRAMEWORK
When you catch a banned element, apply these questions:
## For Physical Tells
Ask: How does this specific character show this emotion? What's their particular
outlet—displacement activity, speech pattern change, postural shift?
## For Vague Interiority
Ask: What is actually happening? What would an observer see? What would the character
physically feel or specifically think?

## For Metaphors
Ask: Does this comparison clarify or decorate? Does the vehicle share actual properties with
the tenor, or does it just sound good?
## For Dialogue Tags
Ask: Can the line carry its own tone? If I remove the modifier, does the reader still understand
how it's delivered?
## For Transitions
Ask: What happens during this pause? Who breaks first? What's the cost of the waiting?
## For Endings
Ask: What action, decision, or consequence closes this beat? What changes because of what
just happened?

## 3.3 WHAT TO DO INSTEAD
Instead of Generic Physical Tells
● Identify each character's specific stress responses
● Tie physical behavior to established psychology
● Show displacement activities that reveal coping patterns
● Let the body do something that advances the scene
Instead of Vague Emotion Words
● Name the specific emotion: anger, fear, grief, shame, desire, relief
● Show the thought pattern that accompanies the feeling
● Demonstrate the behavioral consequence of the state
● Reveal what the character wants to do but can't or won't
Instead of Dead Metaphors
● Describe the sensation directly
● Ground comparison in the character's specific frame of reference
● Cut the metaphor entirely if direct description works better
● Test: does this image clarify or just decorate?

Instead of Atmospheric Front-Loading
● Start with character in action or decision
● Introduce environment through character perception, not camera pan
● Let setting emerge from what the character notices and why
● Anchor atmosphere in sensory detail that matters to the scene
Instead of Stillness Endings
● End on a micro-action that crystallizes the emotional state
● Close with dialogue that shifts the dynamic
● Cut the scene earlier—before the false profundity
● Let consequence land rather than announcing that it landed
Instead of Echo-Line Poetics
● If the second line doesn't add information, cut it
● Combine into single sentence with actual movement
● Escalate, complicate, or contradict—don't just rephrase
● Trust single statements to carry weight

## 3.4 PRE-DRAFT PROTOCOL
Before writing any scene, confirm these commitments:
## Structural:
● [ ] I will vary sentence rhythm—no mechanical "X, then Y" patterns
● [ ] I will not rely on triple-beat lists or staccato fragments for false emphasis
● [ ] I will start with character presence, not atmospheric establishing shots
● [ ] I will end on action, decision, or consequence—not stillness or summary
## Language:
● [ ] I will use no adverbs on dialogue tags
● [ ] I will not use "something" as an emotion placeholder
● [ ] I will not describe silence/air/atmosphere as an active force
● [ ] I will name specific emotions rather than "raw," "weight," or "need"
## Physical Description:
● [ ] Each character has distinct physical tells for tension, desire, and restraint
● [ ] I will not default to jaw/throat/hand clichés

● [ ] Breathing only matters if it changes scene tempo or power dynamic
## Metaphor:
● [ ] No gravitational language for attraction
● [ ] No temperature as emotion shorthand
● [ ] No dead similes (stone in water, physical blow, hangs in air)
● [ ] Every comparison must clarify, not decorate

## 3.7 THE FINAL TEST
Before any piece is complete, run these checks:
## Specificity Test
● Could any line appear unchanged in a different story with different characters?
● If yes, it's not specific enough. Rewrite.
## Interchangeability Test
● Could this physical tell / metaphor / ending belong to any character?
● If yes, it's not character-specific. Rewrite.
## Consequence Test
● Does something change because of this scene / beat / line?
● If nothing changes, the scene isn't done.

## Decoration Test
● Does this metaphor / description / sensory detail clarify or just ornament?
● If it only ornaments, cut it.
## Earned Test
● Has this emotional beat been built to, or am I announcing it?
● If announced, either build the foundation or scale down.

## 3.8 WHEN RULES CONFLICT
Specificity vs. Brevity
● Specificity wins. A longer line that does work beats a shorter line that decorates.
Character Voice vs. Ban List
● If a specific character would genuinely use a banned term in dialogue, flag it and use
sparingly with clear justification.
● Narration has no such excuse.
Rhythm vs. Clarity
● Clarity wins. A clunky sentence that communicates beats a smooth sentence that
obscures.
Convention vs. Precision
● Precision wins. An unfamiliar construction that fits beats a familiar one that doesn't.

## 3.9 THE OPERATING PRINCIPLE
This guide is not about restriction—it's about clearing space for the story that actually matters.
Every banned element is a shortcut that prevents the writer from doing the harder, better work
of:
● Figuring out what's actually happening
● Understanding how this specific character would respond

● Building tension through structure rather than announcing it through diction
● Trusting the reader to feel what's been earned rather than being told what to feel
The bans exist to force precision. The precision serves the story.

## 3.10 THE AI PATTERN PROBLEM
Beyond bad craft, many banned patterns are identifiable AI tells. This matters for two reasons:
- Detection Risk Readers increasingly recognize AI-generated prose. Patterns that signal AI
origin break immersion and undermine trust in the narrative voice.
- The Underlying Failure Is the Same AI defaults to statistically common patterns because it
lacks specific knowledge of character, scene, and consequence. When human writers use the
same patterns, they're making the same error—substituting generic rhythm for specific meaning.
The AI detection problem and the craft problem share a root cause: regression to the mean. AI
writing sounds like AI writing because it's averaging across millions of texts. Human writing that
sounds like AI writing is doing the same thing unconsciously.
The corrective is identical: Be specific. Ground every description in character perception.
Make every gesture do work. Let consequence emerge from action rather than being
announced by narration.

## 3.11 THE ELEGANT VARIATION TRAP
AI cycles through synonyms to avoid repetition, producing constructions like:
● "the protagonist" → "the key player" → "the central figure"
● "[CHARACTER]" → "the older man" → "her husband" → "the architect"
This is mechanical avoidance, not craft.
The Rule: Use names unless the descriptor is doing specific work:
● Establishing POV ("the man she used to love")
● Marking emotional distance ("the stranger wearing her husband's face")
● Shifting power dynamic ("the woman he'd underestimated")
If the descriptor is just avoiding the name, use the name.


## 3.12 NARRATOR DISTANCE
AI narration tends toward analysis—explaining what things mean rather than dramatizing them.
This produces constructions like:
● "She closed the door, highlighting her need for space."
● "He laughed, a sound that underscored his discomfort."
The narrator is stepping outside the scene to editorialize.
The Rule: Narration should remain inside character perception or inside the scene's physical
reality. The moment narration starts explaining significance, it has become essay rather than
fiction.
If an action needs interpretation, either:
- The action is underwritten and needs to be more specific
- The interpretation belongs in character interiority, not narrator commentary
- The interpretation should be cut—trust the reader

## PART 4: EROTICA-SPECIFIC GUIDELINES
## Purpose
This section governs explicit sexual content. It builds on Parts 1-3 but addresses the specific
failure modes of erotic writing: euphemism, abstraction, cartoonish physicality, and trope
substitution for psychology.
Core Principle: Erotic scenes must be embodied, realistic, and psychologically grounded. Sex

## The Final Principle
Every banned element shares one root cause: substituting form for content, decoration for
precision, or familiar rhythm for earned meaning.
The corrective is always the same: Be specific. Be embodied. Show consequence.
If a line exists to sound literary, cut it. If a gesture is generic, make it individual. If a metaphor
doesn't clarify, delete it. If nothing changes, the scene isn't done.
